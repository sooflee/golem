The method

How it works

0The big idea: play the tournament 100,000 times

You can't know what will happen at one World Cup — it's played once, and luck matters. So we ask an answerable question: if this exact tournament were replayed thousands of times, how often would each team win?

That's a Monte Carlo simulation. Simulate one match realistically — with the right randomness — and you can play all 103 matches (we skip the dead-rubber 3rd-place playoff), repeat 100,000 times, and just count. Win 16,400 of 100,000 → a 16.4% title chance.

The whole challenge is three things: (1) how good is each team, (2) how "how good" becomes a result including luck, and (3) the tournament structure. The rest is counting.

1How good is each team? (Elo ratings)

Every team gets an Elo rating — the system invented for chess. You gain points for winning (more for beating a strong team), and the gap between two ratings predicts the result, built so a 400-point gap ≈ a 10-to-1 favorite:

win_expectancy = 1 / (1 + 10^(−(rating_A − rating_B)/400))

Equal teams → 0.5; a 200-point edge → ~76%. Hosts get a small home bump.

Why win-expectancy, not raw points? Different sites publish Elo on different scales. This formula always maps a gap to the same probability, so the model works with any ratings.

2Simulating one match

We simulate the score, not just win/lose — the group stage ranks by goal difference. We turn each side's win-expectancy into expected goals, then draw a random scoreline from a Poisson distribution (the standard model for counting rare scattered events — goals, raindrops, typos per page):

goals_A ~ Poisson( 1.178 × e^( 1.98×(win_exp−0.5)) ) goals_B ~ Poisson( 1.178 × e^(−1.98×(win_exp−0.5)) )

Two even teams average 1.178 each; a favorite's average rises. The Dixon-Coles correction nudges low scores so the draw rate matches reality.

3A worked example: Spain vs Croatia

Spain (rating 2090) vs Croatia (rating 1930), neutral venue. Exactly what the engine does:

Step A — rating gap

2090 − 1930 = 161 points in Spain's favor

Step B — turn the gap into a win expectancy

win_exp = 1 / (1 + 10^(−161/400)) = 0.716 → on strength alone, Spain is about a 72% favorite

Step C — turn that into expected goals

λ(Spain) = 1.178 × e^( 1.98×(0.716−0.5)) = 1.81 goals λ(Croatia) = 1.178 × e^(−1.98×(0.716−0.5)) = 0.77 goals

Step D — roll the dice (Poisson + draw correction)

61%Spain win
26%draw
14%Croatia win

Most likely exact score: Spain 2–0 Croatia. Croatia still avoids defeat 39% of the time — that's the randomness from Step 4. A tournament is just this, 103 times, repeated many times over.

4The key step: measuring how random football is

The two numbers above (1.178 and 1.98) control how much a rating edge really decides a match versus how much is luck. Guess wrong and the model gets over-confident (a bank once put one team at 26% to win it all) or under-confident. So we measure them from 24,787 real internationals since 2000 (recent football only), finding the settings that make the results that actually happened most likely (maximum likelihood). The verdict on football's built-in randomness:

Matchup (by rating)WinDrawLose
Even (50/50)34%32%34%
Slight edge (60%)46%30%24%
Clear favorite (70%)59%26%15%
Big favorite (80%)70%21%9%
Huge favorite (90%)81%15%5%
This is why upsets are common. Even a clear favorite loses or draws ~40% of single games. A high title number is only justified if it comes from a genuine rating gap — not from pretending matches are more predictable than 24,787 games show. (A model that reaches, say, 26% by under-estimating this randomness is over-confident; here the randomness is fit to the data, so any concentration comes from the gaps themselves — which is why the slider's pure-model end can be high yet still honest.)

5Trusting the betting market (calibration & the slider)

The betting market is the best-calibrated forecast there is — millions of people with real money, reacting to injuries and news. You can calibrate to either of two markets (the buttons above the slider): a sportsbook futures board (ESPN) or the Kalshi exchange. Their prices sum to over 100% — that overage (the "vig", ~18% for the sportsbook but only ~5% on the peer-to-peer exchange) we strip out. Then we calibrate: nudge each team's rating until our simulated title odds match that blended target (market + our model, at the slider's mix).

The slider at the top blends the two: 0% = our pure Elo model (free to disagree with the market, Goldman-style), 100% = the pure market. The recommended default is 85% market / 15% model — lean on the market (historically the best single forecast) while keeping the model as a hedge.

6Does it actually work? (Backtesting)

A forecast you never check is just an opinion. We tested the match engine on 552 real World Cup matches (1990–2022), predicting each from the two teams' reconstructed Elo beforehand:

8.2%better than a naive baseline (log-loss)
73%favorite correctly picked (decisive games)
0.977log-loss vs baseline 1.065

We score with log-loss, which rewards being confident and right and punishes confident-but-wrong, so you can't game it by hedging. The randomness we measured on ordinary games was also near-optimal for World Cup games — so we didn't tune the model to its own data.

What this does and doesn't test. The backtest scores the match engine — how a rating gap becomes a result — using each team's reconstructed Elo at the time, with no market data and no ensemble. It validates the simulation machinery that carries every number here through the bracket, but not the market calibration or the 85/15 blend (Section 5): those have no historical betting odds to test against.

What it can't do (being honest)

  • Ratings are fixed at kickoff — no reaction to a mid-tournament injury.
  • Ratings are a single pre-tournament snapshot from one provider (eloratings.net) — a different rating system would shift the edges somewhat.
  • Group tiebreakers are simplified versus FIFA's full head-to-head rules.
  • We validated the engine and randomness, but not the exact blend weight — that needs historical betting odds we don't have. (Hence the slider: judge for yourself.)
